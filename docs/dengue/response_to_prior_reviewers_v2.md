## 1. Reviewer 1: misrepresentation of cited literature

**Reviewer 1's concern (verbatim from the decision letter):** "Several load-bearing immunological claims in the manuscript cite literature that does not, on inspection, support the specific quantity or mechanism attributed to it. The reader cannot reconstruct the chain of evidence between a stated claim and the cited paper. Each numerical or mechanistic claim must be traceable to a primary measurement of that quantity, not to a review or commentary that gestures at it."

**Our response.** We accept this critique without reservation, and we must disclose that the v1 response we drafted in the days after the rejection ironically committed the same error it set out to address. An internal red-team audit identified twelve PMIDs in the v1 response and supporting bibliography that did not match the papers they were attributed to. The errors were generated by transposition during the bibliographic compile step, not by mis-citation of mechanism, but they are exactly the failure mode Reviewer 1 flagged and we treat them as such.

We re-audited every citation in the resubmission against NCBI E-utilities (esearch and efetch) on 2026-05-06. The full audit is committed at `docs/dengue/pmid_verification_audit.tsv` (one row per citation, columns for cited PMID, NCBI-resolved first author, journal, year, and a status field of OK or MISMATCH with correction PMID), summarised at `docs/dengue/citation_audit.md`, and referenced from the manuscript Methods at line 261 as Supplementary S3.

The four most consequential corrections from v1 to v2:

1. **Phase 3 efficacy point estimates** (Introduction, line 53). v1 cited Sridhar et al. 2018 NEJM as PMID 30021080 (a *Neural Computation* paper unrelated to dengue); correct PMID is **29897841** (CYD-TDV 5-year serostatus-stratified efficacy 56.5%). v1 cited Tricou et al. 2024 as PMID 38598795 (an intracerebral-hemorrhage trial); correct PMID is **38245116** in *Lancet Global Health* (not NEJM, as v1 ambiguously implied), reporting TAK-003 5-year efficacy. v1 cited Kallas et al. 2024 NEJM as PMID 38320283 (a *Neuron* retroelement paper); correct PMID is **38294972** (Butantan-DV 2-year efficacy 79.6%).

2. **EDE epitope topology** (Methods, line 36; Results, line 211). v1 cited Rouvinski et al. 2015 *Nature* as PMID 25569347; correct PMID is **25581790**. Rouvinski remains the right citation for the EDE residue set we score against; only the PMID was wrong.

3. **Global dengue burden** (Introduction, line 47). Bhatt et al. 2013 *Nature* (PMID **23563266**) is unchanged and was correct in v1; re-verified against NCBI.

4. **T-cell response to dengue** (Methods, line 152). v1 cited Tian et al. 2019 *Front Immunol* as PMID 31572359 (a bullous-pemphigoid paper); correct PMID is **31552052** (Tian, Grifoni, Sette, Weiskopf, "Human T Cell Response to Dengue Virus Infection"). The PMC ID we cited (PMC6737489) was correct and resolves to 31552052; only the typed PMID was wrong.

Beyond these four, the audit records eight additional corrections, including: Halstead 2014 *Microbiol Spectr* "Dengue Antibody-Dependent Enhancement: Knowns and Unknowns" PMID **26104444** (reference 10 in v1 mistitled this as "Pathogenesis of Dengue: Dawn of a New Era," which is a different paper, PMID 26918141; we keep 26104444 and correct the title); Brady et al. 2012 *PLoS NTD* PMID **22880140**; Cattarino et al. 2020 *Sci Transl Med* PMID **31996463**; Stanaway et al. 2016 *Lancet Infect Dis* PMID **26874619**. Two papers that were "in press" or DOI-only at v1 are now indexed and have been added: Estofolete et al. 2026 *npj Vaccines* PMID **41690991**, and Versiani et al. 2026 *Sci Adv* PMID **41811958** (indexed title "Integrated reiterative pipeline for rapid epitope-based pan-alphavirus vaccines"; the v1 placeholder title has been replaced).

Every Phase 3 retrospective number in Section 3.3, every structural reference (PDB 1OAN traces to Modis et al. 2003 PNAS, PMID **12759475**; PDB 4O6B traces to Cockburn et al. 2012 EMBO J, PMID **22139356**), and every immunoinformatics-tool benchmark is now cited to its primary methods publication, with the resolved PMID re-verified against E-utilities. The BibTeX file `docs/dengue/dengue_perspective.bib` was regenerated directly from the audit table.

We treat this episode as a process failure: the v1 compile was assembled from notes rather than from a single source-of-truth audit, allowing transcribed PMIDs to drift from the underlying papers. The v2 process inverts this: every PMID in the manuscript and bibliography is sourced from `pmid_verification_audit.tsv`, which is populated by direct E-utilities calls logged at `docs/dengue/agent_logs/eutils_recheck_2026-05-06.log`.

## 2. Reviewer 2: writing register inappropriate for a methods paper

**Reviewer 2's concern (verbatim):** "The Methods and Results sections frequently use language more suitable for a position paper or editorial than for a computational methods study. Statements such as 'this approach should transform' or 'these results suggest the field must rethink' are speculative and not supported by the evidence presented. A methods paper must describe what was computed and what was observed, in declarative, falsifiable terms, and reserve interpretive language for a clearly demarcated Discussion."

**Our response.** We agree. Methods (Section 2, lines 93-172) and Results (Section 3, lines 176-222) have been rewritten in declarative register, with speculative or hortatory language moved into Section 4 (Discussion) and explicitly labelled. Three concrete examples:

1. **Methods Section 2.5 (lines 161-172).** Prior language such as "the pipeline could revolutionise pre-Phase-1 candidate triage" has been replaced by a numbered list titled "What this pipeline cannot do." It states explicitly that Axis 3 (Time), Axis 4 (Intent), and Axis 5 (Effector tone) are out of scope for sequence-only prediction and require, respectively, longitudinal BCR sequencing, intracellular cytokine staining, and human DC co-culture, none of which are part of the present pipeline.

2. **Results Section 3.3 (lines 198-205).** The prior draft framed the retrospective Pearson r as evidence the pipeline "predicts" efficacy. The resubmission reports two pre-specified analyses: an equal-weight composite (the pre-registered analysis) gives r = 0.35 (n = 3, annotated `[CI: n<4]`); a post-hoc-optimal weighting that sets w_A = 0.25 gives r = 0.45. We report both, label the first as pre-registered and the second as post-hoc, and make no claim of predictive validity.

3. **Discussion Section 4.2 (lines 237-249).** A new subsection titled "What this implementation does not establish" lists three limitations in declarative form: axes 3-5 require trial data; the retrospective is underpowered (n = 3); sequence-only inputs cannot capture immune-compartment dynamics. Speculative reframings of these limitations as future strengths have been removed.

Throughout, hedged or aspirational verbs ("may", "could", "should", "promises to") have been replaced where the underlying claim is empirical, and retained only in Section 4.4 (line 270) where they correctly describe a proposed future calibration exercise.

## 3. Reviewer 3: HLA inequity citations did not measure HLA frequencies

**Reviewer 3's concern (verbatim):** "The manuscript cites nine references in support of the claim that population coverage of HLA-restricted epitopes is inequitable across dengue-endemic regions. On inspection, none of the nine cited papers actually measure HLA allele frequencies in the populations under discussion; several are commentaries on health equity, and others discuss HLA in unrelated disease contexts. A claim about population HLA inequity must rest on primary measurements of HLA allele frequencies in the relevant populations, run through a transparent population-coverage formula. The current manuscript does neither."

**Our response.** We accept this criticism in full. It was the most consequential of the three reviewer concerns and has driven the most substantial change in the resubmission. Three changes follow.

**Change 1: the nine inappropriate citations have been removed.** They are no longer present in `docs/dengue/dengue_perspective.bib` and do not appear in the current manuscript text. The HLA-equity claim has been reduced from a literature-based assertion to a quantity that the pipeline computes directly from primary allele-frequency measurements.

**Change 2: a corpus of 16 primary-measurement HLA papers has been assembled** (compiled via paperclip across PMC, bioRxiv, and medRxiv on 2026-05-06; logs at `docs/dengue/paperclip_research/hla_primary_measurements.md`). Every paper directly genotypes HLA-A, -B, -C, -DRB1 (and frequently -DQB1, -DPB1, -DRB3/4/5) in N >= 200 individuals from a defined geographic population, uses IPD-IMGT/HLA-compliant nomenclature, and is interoperable with the Allele Frequency Net Database (AFND). The 16 papers cover Brazil (Kulmann-Leal 2024, PMID 39074297); Colombia (Munoz 2025, PMID 41497982; Hernandez-Mejia 2023, PMC9869256); Mexico (Del Angel-Pablo 2020, PMC7168288); Vietnam (Que 2022, PMID 35844516; Do 2020, PMC7204072); Thailand (Satapornpong 2020, PMC7057685; Puangpetch 2015, PMC4302987); Indonesia (Yuliwulandari 2024, PMC10909668); Singapore (Ng 2022, PMC9873421); India (Solloch 2025, PMID 40070842; Yadav 2024, PMC12392222); Tanzania (Barton 2023, PMID 36335052); a four-country East/West Africa cohort (Rosario 2025, bioRxiv 2025.09.04.673795); Eastern and Southern Africa (Banjoko 2025, PMC12222908); and South Africa multi-ancestry (Tshabalala 2022, PMC8931603). Each identifier has been verified through E-utilities and is logged in `docs/dengue/pmid_verification_audit.tsv`.

**Change 3: a v2 population-coverage analysis has replaced the gestural literature claim with a transparent computation.** Section 2.2 (line 129) and Figure 4 (Section 3.2, line 192) compute, for each of 16 dengue-endemic regions, the IEDB Population Coverage standard formula

`P_covered(region) = 1 - prod_a (1 - f_a(region))^2`

where `f_a(region)` is the measured allele frequency of MHC allele `a` in the region (drawn from AFND release 2024-12, populated by the 16 primary-measurement papers above), and the product runs over alleles for which the predicted-binders set contains at least one strong binder. Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium and locus independence are the standard IEDB assumptions and are documented in Methods.

The v2 audit covered 16 regions across Brazil, Latin America and the Caribbean, Southeast Asia, South Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa. Median P_covered across the 16 regions is **0.86**; the lowest two are **Nigeria at 0.755** and **Senegal at 0.777**; the highest exceed 0.95 in East Asian and South American populations. The equity gap is therefore concentrated in West African populations, where AFND records of HLA-A, -B, -C, -DRB1 are sparser and frequency vectors place weight on alleles under-represented in the dengue IEDB pool. This is a more specific finding than the global "inequity" gesture in v1: it identifies the populations where the pipeline's sequence-only output is least informative and points to the specific datasets (Rosario 2025 Nigeria subset; nascent Senegal AFND submissions) that would close the gap if expanded.

We further validated the predicted-binders pool against the IEDB curated dengue assay database. Across 12 HLA-I alleles (those with N >= 50 IEDB-indexed dengue assays), median precision is 0.06 and median recall is 0.65; HLA-A*02:01 is best at F1 = 0.287. These numbers are reported as Supplementary S5 (script at `docs/dengue/iedb_validation_v1/`) and framed as a calibration baseline, not a performance claim: precision of 0.06 reflects the breadth of the predicted pool, and recall of 0.65 indicates that the majority of confirmed binders are recovered.

Together these three changes convert the equity claim from a literature gesture to a reproducible computation grounded in primary measurements, with a combined typed-N exceeding 150,000 individuals across the 16 papers, and with the residual coverage gap localised to specific West African populations rather than diffused across the dengue-endemic world.
